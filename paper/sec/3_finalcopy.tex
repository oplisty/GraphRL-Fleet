\section{Experiment}
\subsection{Experiment Setup}
This section introduces the experimental setup from four aspects: the experimental environment, comparison methods, evaluation metrics, and implementation details. Overall, our goal is to systematically evaluate the differences among heuristic baselines, the Q-learning hyper-heuristic, and the offline MILP method in terms of task completion quality, running cost, and scalability within a unified dynamic new energy logistics scheduling environment.
\subsubsection{Environment Setup}
The environment is built on a graph-based road network and explicitly models a limited number of vehicles, dynamically released tasks, vehicle battery and load constraints, charging-station resources, and task deadlines. Our experiments support three different scales with the following configurations:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{small:}  includes 5 vehicles, 30 dynamic tasks, 2 charging stations, and 25 road nodes. The map size is \(30\times 30\), and the simulation horizon is 180. This scale is mainly used for quickly verifying algorithm effectiveness and training convergence behavior.
    
    \item \textbf{medium:}  includes 10 vehicles, 100 dynamic tasks, 4 charging stations, and 60 road nodes. The map size is \(50\times 50\), and the simulation horizon is 300. This scale is used to test the stability of different methods under higher task density and more complex charging pressure.
    
    \item \textbf{large:} includes 20 vehicles, 300 dynamic tasks, 8 charging stations, and 120 road nodes. The map size is \(80\times 80\), and the simulation horizon is 480. In addition, each charging station is equipped with 3 chargers by default. This scale is mainly used to evaluate the scalability and robustness of different methods in complex dynamic scenarios.
\end{itemize}

In addition to the scale factor, charging decisions can significantly affect vehicle availability, task delay, and the overall score. 
Therefore, the experiments also consider two charging strategies(\text{optimal\_station} and \text{nearest\_station}). 
The former jointly considers travel distance and station load when selecting a charging station, while the latter always gives priority to the nearest charging station.

\subsubsection{Evaluation Metric}
To comprehensively evaluate the performance of different methods in the dynamic new energy logistics scenario, we report experimental metrics from two aspects: task effectiveness and operating cost. The definitions of these metrics are given below.

\noindent \textbf{Task effectiveness metrics:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Final Score:} For each completed task \(\tau_i\), its reward is defined as
    \[
    \mathrm{Score}(\tau_i)=
    \max\left(
    0,
    R_0
    -\alpha \cdot \min\left(\frac{d_i}{D_{\mathrm{ref}}},1\right)
    -P_{\mathrm{late}}\cdot \mathbb{I}(t_i^{\mathrm{finish}}>d_i^{\mathrm{deadline}})
    \right).
    \]
    Here, \(R_0\) is the base reward, \(d_i\) is the service travel distance of task \(i\), \(D_{\mathrm{ref}}\) is the reference distance used for normalization, \(\alpha\) is the normalized distance-cost weight, and \(P_{\mathrm{late}}\) is the penalty for late completion. In our implementation, \(R_0=100\), \(\alpha=30\), and \(P_{\mathrm{late}}=60\). Unfinished expired tasks receive an additional failure penalty \(P_{\mathrm{fail}}\), and the final score is
    \[
    \mathrm{FinalScore}=
    \sum_{i\in\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{completed}}}\mathrm{Score}(\tau_i)
    -P_{\mathrm{fail}}\cdot \lvert\mathcal{T}_{\mathrm{expired}}\rvert
    +S_{\mathrm{partial}},
    \]
    where \(P_{\mathrm{fail}}=60\) by default and \(S_{\mathrm{partial}}\) denotes optional partial credit for collaborative tasks. This scoring function does not directly penalize task waiting time; instead, it considers completion, normalized service distance, and whether the task is completed after its deadline.
    
    \item \textbf{Completed Tasks:} The number of tasks completed by the end of the simulation.
    \item \textbf{Expired Tasks:} The number of tasks that pass their deadlines and remain unfinished by the end of the simulation.
    \item \textbf{Total Tardiness:} This metric is used for offline MILP result analysis. It is defined as the sum of task delays over all tasks
    \[
    \sum_n \max(0, \mathrm{arr}_n-d_n).
    \]
    If a task is completed before its deadline, its tardiness is recorded as 0.
\end{itemize}

\noindent \textbf{Operating cost metrics:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Total Distance:} The sum of the travel distances of all vehicles.
    
    \item \textbf{Steps:} the number of discrete time steps from the initial time to the end of the simulation in online simulation. 
    \item \textbf{Makespan:}the maximum completion time after all vehicles finish service and return to the depot in offline MILP.
    \item \textbf{Charging Burden:} We report two statistics: 1) \textbf{the number of charging triggers}, which measures how often the system enters the charging process; and 2) \textbf{the average queue load}, which measures congestion caused by competition for charging resources.
\end{itemize}

\noindent \textbf{Training-stage metrics:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Total Reward:} The sum of immediate rewards over all time steps in a single training episode.
    \item \textbf{Eval Score Mean:} The mean final score obtained by applying the greedy policy over several evaluation episodes after each training round. This metric is used to measure the stable performance of the learned policy, rather than relying only on a single training trajectory.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experiment Results}
The focus of this experimental analysis includes the performance differences of fixed heuristic baselines across different problem scales, the advantages of the Q-learning hyper-heuristic over the best baseline, the convergence characteristics of Q-learning during training, and the impact of charging strategy design on overall system performance. For ease of explanation, this paper mainly discusses the following four figures: Figure 1 presents the main results comparison of fixed heuristic baselines across three problem scales; Figure 2 shows the overall comparison between Q-learning and the best baseline; Figure 3 illustrates the convergence curves of Q-learning under different training scales; and Figure 4 reports the results of the charging strategy ablation experiment.
\subsubsection{Baseline Comparison}
\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../experiments/figures/main/fig_baseline_multi_scale.pdf}
    \caption{Main comparison of heuristic baselines across three problem scales. It shows the performance of three fixed heuristic scheduling strategies (Nearest, EDF, and Heaviest) under three task scales: small, medium, and large. The comparison metrics include Final Score, Completed Tasks, Expired Tasks, and Total Distance. In this set of experiments, all baselines adopt the optimal station charging strategy to reduce the interference of differences in charging strategies on the main comparison results.}
    \label{fig:baseline_multi_scale}
\end{figure}
Overall, as the scenario scale expands from small to medium and large, the system load, task density, vehicle conflicts, and competition for charging resources gradually intensify, making the performance differences among different fixed heuristic strategies increasingly evident. In the small scenario, the system pressure is relatively low. Nearest and EDF both achieve good performance in terms of Final Score and Completed Tasks, while Heaviest performs relatively poorly. This indicates that both local path efficiency and task deadline constraints can effectively support scheduling decisions at a small scale. In the medium scenario, scheduling pressure increases, and EDF generally outperforms the other baselines in Final Score and Completed Tasks. Nearest remains competitive, whereas Heaviest performs worse in Expired Tasks due to its insufficient responsiveness to urgent tasks. In the large scenario, the high-load environment further amplifies the limitations of fixed rules. Although EDF and Nearest still clearly outperform Heaviest, both Expired Tasks and Total Distance increase significantly, indicating that a single heuristic rule struggles to balance path efficiency, task urgency, and resource competition simultaneously. Moreover, rule differences accumulate in large-scale scenarios, leading to more pronounced performance divergence.
\subsubsection{Comparison with Q-learning}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\linewidth]{../experiments/figures/main/fig_qlearning_vs_best_baseline.pdf}
    \caption{Overall comparison between Q-learning and the best baseline}
    \label{fig:qlearning_vs_baseline}
\end{figure}

In the small scenario, Q-HH-Small performs particularly well, achieving a Final Score of about 2554.64, around 28 Completed Tasks, and almost 0 Expired Tasks, indicating that Q-learning can quickly learn effective rule-switching strategies under a smaller state space and lower resource-conflict conditions. In contrast, Q-HH-Mixed obtains a slightly lower Final Score of about 2424.37, with a similar number of Completed Tasks but 2 Expired Tasks, suggesting a trade-off between generalization and specialization. In the medium scenario, Q-HH-Medium also shows strong competitiveness, with a Final Score of about 2417.17, around 52 Completed Tasks, and 32 Expired Tasks. Although Q-learning may not outperform the best fixed baseline on every individual metric, it can dynamically switch among heuristic rules to balance path efficiency, task urgency, and resource constraints, thereby maintaining strong overall performance. The inclusion of Q-HH-Mixed further demonstrates the cross-scale generalization ability of the hyper-heuristic: while it sacrifices some peak performance compared with scale-specific models, it learns transferable rule-selection patterns. Overall, Figure 2 shows that Q-learning can compete with or even surpass the best fixed heuristic, with its main value lying in state-dependent heuristic selection rather than the creation of a single new heuristic rule.
\subsubsection{Training Convergence}
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../experiments/figures/main/fig_qlearning_training_curves.pdf}
    \caption{Convergence curves of Q-learning under different training scales}
    \label{fig:qlearning_convergence}
\end{figure}
Overall, Figure 3 shows that Q-learning demonstrates good convergence in the current scheduling problem, but its training performance changes significantly as task scale and environmental complexity increase. The small model, with a smaller state space, simpler task interactions, and fewer resource conflicts, can reach a high reward level at an early stage. Its smoothed training curve shows relatively small fluctuations, and eval score mean gradually stabilizes in the middle and later stages, indicating that tabular Q-learning can more easily learn a stable near-optimal strategy. In contrast, the training process of the medium model is more complex. The raw curves of both Total Reward and Eval Score Mean fluctuate more strongly; although the smoothed curves still show an overall upward trend, convergence is slower and mid-stage fluctuations are more pronounced. This reflects the stronger coupling among battery constraints, path selection, task deadlines, and competition for charging resources. The Mixed model reflects the characteristics of generalization-oriented training: it has larger early-stage fluctuations and usually converges more slowly than the specifically trained small model, because it needs to switch among scenarios of different scales and learn more general rule-selection patterns. However, as training progresses, its evaluation curve gradually becomes stable, suggesting that it has learned certain cross-scenario transferable patterns. Therefore, the Q-learning hyper-heuristic is not a random or uncontrollable strategy-switching mechanism, but can gradually form an effective scheduling strategy. Nevertheless, the larger the task scale and the more complex the environment, the greater the training difficulty and instability. Although mixed training sacrifices some convergence speed and peak performance, it provides a feasible path toward building a unified scheduling framework with cross-scale generalization ability.

\subsubsection{Ablation}
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{../experiments/figures/main/fig_charging_ablation.pdf}
    \caption{Results of the charging strategy ablation experiment}
    \label{fig:charging_strategy_ablation}
\end{figure}

Overall, the results show that the optimal charging station strategy generally leads to a higher task completion rate and fewer expired tasks than the nearest charging station strategy. This indicates that choosing the nearest charging station based only on distance can reduce the travel cost of a single trip in the short term, but it may also cause multiple vehicles to gather at the same station, leading to queueing delays and resource congestion. In contrast, the optimal charging station strategy considers travel distance, station load, and charger occupancy together, allowing charging demand to be distributed from a more global perspective and thereby improving the overall operational efficiency of the system.

At the same time, the charging strategy affects not only the performance of fixed heuristic methods, but also the Q-learning hyper-heuristic. Even when the task scheduling layer can dynamically select different rules through the learning mechanism, the allocation of charging resources at the lower level still directly influences the final scheduling outcome. The results show that Q-learning combined with a more reasonable charging strategy usually completes more tasks and reduces task expiration. This suggests that the learning-based scheduling module cannot fully replace the resource allocation module; instead, the two form a collaborative relationship. Task scheduling determines which tasks vehicles should prioritize, while the charging strategy determines how vehicles can maintain their operational capability more efficiently.

It should be noted that charging-wait-related metrics are more suitable for reflecting trend differences between strategies and should not be interpreted as strict absolute waiting times. Even so, the overall trend clearly shows that the optimal charging station strategy has clear advantages in alleviating resource conflicts, reducing concentrated vehicle queues, and improving system coordination. Therefore, charging strategy design is an important factor affecting overall system performance. It should not be treated merely as an auxiliary module, but should be jointly optimized together with the task scheduling strategy.
